# Publishability Assessment

## The headline finding, up front

I did the literature check I owed you back in phase 1, and it changes the novelty picture. In the last few months (arXiv timestamps from January through May 2026), at least four papers have converged on almost exactly this problem, and one of them (SentinelAgent) already does a more rigorous version of the specific mechanism this project builds, with formal proofs and a 516-scenario benchmark. I'm not going to soften this: as originally scoped, "transitive, action-grounded intent fidelity" is not novel enough anymore for USENIX/CCS/NDSS. That doesn't mean stop, it means the pitch and the target need to change, and I'll lay out what I think is still real and defensible below.

## What's already out there

**SentinelAgent** (Patil, arXiv:2604.02767, April 2026) is the closest thing to a direct hit. It's a formal framework, the Delegation Chain Calculus, with seven proven properties for delegation chains: authority narrowing, intent entailment preservation (their P2, probabilistic, everything else deterministic), policy conjunction preservation, forensic reconstructibility, cascade containment, scope-action conformance (their P6, this is action-grounding), and output schema conformance (P7). Their "three-point verification lifecycle" is pre-execution intent check (P2) plus at-execution scope enforcement (P6) plus post-execution output validation (P7), which is architecturally the same shape as this project's pairwise/action-grounding/transitive split, just with an added output-validation stage this project doesn't have. They evaluate on DelegationBench v4 (516 scenarios, 10 attack categories, 13 federal domains), report 100% combined TPR at 0% FPR, and separately report that intent verification alone degrades to 13% TPR under adversarial paraphrasing, exactly the weakness flagged about this project's lexical similarity backend, before fine-tuning an NLI model on 190 labeled examples brought it to 88.3%. They also prove, via a Rice's-theorem analogy plus eight hand-constructed ambiguous delegation pairs, that deterministic intent verification is provably infeasible, which is a stronger and more rigorous version of the false-positive/false-negative argument I made to you in phase 1 from first principles.

**HDP** (Dalugoda, arXiv:2604.04522, April 2026) is, functionally, the Intent Envelope's crypto layer already standardized: a signed, hash-linked, append-only chain of delegation hops verifiable offline with just the issuer's Ed25519 public key, already an IETF Internet-Draft with a public TypeScript SDK. This confirms what I told you in phase 1 (don't oversell the provenance layer, it's not new), except now there's a named, shipping, spec'd protocol doing it, not just prior art like in-toto to differentiate from.

**"Authorization Propagation in Multi-Agent AI Systems"** (Tallam, arXiv:2605.05440, May 2026) formally separates the authorization-propagation problem from prompt injection and defines three sub-problems: transitive delegation (does authority stay bounded and non-accumulating across hops, a scope question, not a semantic-fidelity question), aggregation inference (do individually-authorized accesses combine into something unauthorized), and temporal validity (when should authorization be re-checked as state changes over the workflow's lifetime). It's rigorous, cites real production incidents, and its "aggregation inference" sub-problem (individually benign subtasks combining into a policy violation, cited at 71% attack success across 14 enterprise scenarios in a paper called "Semantic Intent Fragmentation") is close in spirit to this project's cumulative-drift scenario, though it's framed as a data-aggregation-policy problem rather than an intent-fidelity metric.

**"Agent Drift"** (Rath, arXiv:2601.04170, January 2026) coins "semantic drift" for exactly the phenomenon this project calls transitive fidelity loss: "progressive deviation from original intent" as a named, measured category, alongside coordination drift and behavioral drift, with a 12-dimension Agent Stability Index.

Also relevant and worth knowing exist: AIP (invocation-bound capability tokens, Biscuit/Datalog), SEAgent (mandatory access control for multi-agent LLM systems), ShieldAgent (safety policy verification), Agent Behavioral Contracts (drift bounding for single agents), and the OWASP Top 10 for Agentic Applications (December 2025), which already names excessive agency, tool misuse, and scope escalation as top-line categories with the field actively building mitigations.

## What that leaves genuinely open

Reading SentinelAgent closely, its P2/P6 combination checks intent-before and scope-at-execution, but at the level I can see from the abstract, introduction, and the two properties I read in full, it doesn't appear to run a check structurally equivalent to this project's transitive check, comparing the leaf directly against the root rather than chaining pairwise scores. Their forensic reconstructibility property (P4) lets an auditor walk the chain after the fact, but that's reconstruction, not a runtime check that catches drift no individual hop would flag. I read the paper's overview and two of seven properties in detail, not the full text, so I can't rule out that root-vs-leaf checking is covered elsewhere in their evaluation section; that needs a full read before anyone states this as a confirmed gap in a paper. Flagging my own incompleteness here rather than asserting more confidence than I have.

Three things look like real, defensible remaining angles, in order of how much work each needs:

**A systematization angle.** At least six overlapping frameworks (SentinelAgent, HDP, AIP, Authenticated Workflows, FormalJudge, ASTRA) plus several adjacent ones appeared in roughly a five-month window, each solving a piece with a different formalism and no shared benchmark or terminology. Nobody has yet published an independent comparison across them: same benchmark, same attack scenarios, different systems side by side. This is a legitimate USENIX/CCS paper type (a SoK, systematization of knowledge), it's less about inventing a new mechanism and more about being the first to actually cross-test what's been claimed. It's also a lot of work, since it means implementing or obtaining implementations of several systems most of which are not open source.

**An independent adversarial replication angle.** Nobody outside SentinelAgent's own team has stress-tested SentinelAgent's claims, or HDP's, or AIP's. An independent red-team evaluation, using an adversary that wasn't involved in designing the defense, is a legitimate and valued contribution at security venues specifically because self-reported 100% TPR numbers invite scrutiny. This project's existing red-team habit (two real bugs found and fixed by actually attacking its own fidelity engine) is good practice for this, but it would need to attack someone else's published system, not just this one, to count as a research contribution rather than an internal QA exercise.

**A narrower empirical angle inside this project's own transitive check**, if it turns out (after a full read of SentinelAgent and the others) that root-vs-leaf direct comparison genuinely isn't in the current published set: publish a small, honest, well-benchmarked empirical study specifically on "pairwise-chained fidelity checks vs. direct root-to-leaf checks," with a real dataset of constructed drift scenarios, showing where the two disagree and by how much. This is a narrower, more modest claim than "we invented intent verification for agents," but it's the kind of focused, falsifiable empirical question program committees like, if it survives the literature check being done properly first.

## What none of this changes

The engineering work already done here is not wasted, and the framing for a bootcamp capstone or a DEF CON AI Village demo is different from the framing for an academic submission. Nobody at a village demo expects a from-scratch peer-reviewed contribution; they expect a working, well-tested, honestly-scoped system that demonstrates you understand the problem, and independently arriving at a similar architecture to what several concurrent research teams converged on in the same few months is itself a reasonable signal that the problem was framed correctly, not evidence the work here was wasted. The adversarial red-team findings (the resource self-declaration bypass, the two false positives that fixing it created) are real, demonstrable, and yours, regardless of what SentinelAgent's paper claims about its own numbers.

## What changes right now, concretely

Stop describing the transitive, action-grounded fidelity check as novel in any pitch, README, or talk. Cite SentinelAgent, HDP, and the Authorization Propagation paper by name in the README and in the demo's opening framing, both because it's honest and because a technically literate DEF CON audience is increasingly likely to know these papers and will trust the talk more, not less, for citing them first. Reframe the pitch from "we detect intent laundering, nobody else does" to "the field converged on this exact problem in the first half of 2026, here's an independent, open-source, from-scratch implementation with its own red-team findings, and here's specifically where it agrees and disagrees with SentinelAgent's approach." That is a more defensible and, honestly, a more interesting talk than the original pitch, because it lets you show the audience a live, working system instead of a set of claims.

## If you want to pursue this further after the bootcamp

Read SentinelAgent in full (not just the sections I pulled), read HDP's spec, and read the Authorization Propagation paper's related-work section (2.4, "Emerging Agent Authorization Frameworks") in full, since it's explicitly cataloging this exact landscape and will have citations this assessment missed. Then pick one of the three angles above, most likely the systematization or independent-replication angle, since those are legitimate contributions regardless of how good this project's own fidelity engine ends up being. Budget real time for this, it's not a one-week add-on, it's a second project.
